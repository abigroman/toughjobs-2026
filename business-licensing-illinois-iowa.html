Before you start marketing

An LLC gets your business on paper. It doesn't get you licensed.

Every week, we talk to plumbers, roofers, HVAC techs, landscapers, and general contractors who've formed an LLC and assume they're ready to advertise. In Illinois and Iowa, many trades need more: a state or local license, a contractor registration, a permit, or proof of insurance — depending on the trade and where the work happens.

An LLC alone does not make a home service business legally ready to advertise or operate.

  • Many trades need state licenses too
  • Cities often require separate registration
  • Insurance and bonding are frequently required
Set the record straight

An LLC does not automatically make a contractor legal to operate.

An LLC (or corporation) is a legal business structure. It affects liability, taxes, and how your business is organized — it does not grant you a trade license, a city registration, or permission to perform regulated work.

✓

What an LLC does

Creates a separate legal entity, can offer liability protection, and organizes how the business is taxed.

×

What an LLC doesn't do

It doesn't license you to do plumbing, electrical, roofing, or other regulated trade work.

×

It doesn't cover local rules

City and county permits, registrations, and inspections are separate from state business formation.

×

It doesn't include insurance

General liability, workers' comp, and bonding are arranged separately, based on your trade and jurisdiction.

The full picture

Common requirements for home service businesses.

Depending on your trade and location, some combination of these typically applies.

✓

Business formation

LLC or corporation registration, a DBA / assumed name if you operate under a different name, and foreign entity registration if you work across state lines.

✓

Tax registration

Registering with the Illinois Department of Revenue or Iowa Department of Revenue, and understanding sales/use tax or contractor tax rules that may apply to your work.

✓

Contractor registration or license

Illinois handles most trades locally, aside from plumbing and roofing. Iowa requires state construction contractor registration above a $2,000/year threshold, plus separate trade licenses for electrical and plumbing/mechanical work.

✓

Local permits & city registration

Cities and counties often require their own registration, permits, and inspections — on top of any state requirement.

✓

Insurance & bonding

General liability, workers' compensation, commercial auto, and license or contractor bonds where required.

✓

Trade-specific certification

Some work — pesticide application, lead-safe renovation, asbestos abatement, fire protection — requires its own certification beyond a general contractor credential.

Illinois contractor licensing

Illinois home service business licensing overview.

Illinois does not issue one statewide general contractor license that covers every trade. Instead, licensing is a mix of two state-level credentials and a patchwork of city and county rules.

State-level licenses

What Illinois licenses directly

  • Plumbing — licensed statewide by the Illinois Department of Public Health (IDPH) under the Illinois Plumbing License Law.
  • Roofing — licensed statewide by the Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation (IDFPR) under the Roofing Industry Licensing Act, with Limited and Unlimited license classes.
  • All other trades — general contracting, electrical, HVAC, and most specialties — have no statewide license. They're regulated city by city and county by county.

Local & other requirements

What else typically applies

  • Business registration with the Illinois Department of Revenue.
  • City contractor registration or licensing — for example, Chicago's Department of Buildings licenses general, electrical, and plumbing contractors separately, in classes based on project value.
  • Many suburbs and downstate cities require their own contractor registration, application fee, and proof of insurance, even where the state doesn't license the trade.
  • Pesticide application for lawn care or landscaping generally requires a separate applicator license through the state's agriculture regulator.
Iowa contractor registration

Iowa home service business licensing overview.

Iowa also has no single statewide "general contractor license," but it does require broad construction contractor registration once you cross a low earnings threshold.

State-level registration

Construction contractor registration

  • Anyone earning $2,000 or more per year from construction work in Iowa must register as a Construction Contractor with the Iowa Department of Inspections, Appeals, and Licensing (DIAL) — a $50 annual fee, renewed yearly.
  • "Construction" is defined broadly under Iowa Code Chapter 91C — it covers carpentry, electrical, plumbing, roofing, painting, landscaping, and more.
  • Out-of-state contractors face additional requirements, including a $25,000 surety bond and registration with the Iowa Department of Revenue.

Separate trade licenses

Beyond contractor registration

  • Plumbing, mechanical & HVAC/refrigeration — licensed by the Iowa Plumbing and Mechanical Systems Board, generally requiring liability insurance and a surety bond.
  • Electrical — licensed by the Iowa Electrical Examining Board.
  • Cities like Des Moines, Cedar Rapids, and Davenport can layer on their own local licensing and permit rules on top of state registration.

Don't skip city hall

Local permits, city registration, and inspections.

State-level rules are only half the picture. City and county requirements are often just as important — and easy to miss.

✓

Trade permits

Plumbing, electrical, roofing, HVAC, and structural work commonly require a project-specific permit from the local building department.

✓

Structural & exterior work

Decks, fences, additions, and demolition often need their own permits and inspections, even for small projects.

✓

Utility & site work

Sewer work, right-of-way work, and signage frequently require separate city or county approval.

✓

Contractor registration

Many cities require contractors to register locally and show proof of insurance before pulling any permit — regardless of state licensing status.

Protect the business

Insurance, bonding, and workers' compensation.

Insurance requirements vary by trade, city, and whether you have employees — but a few types come up again and again.

✓

General liability

Covers property damage or injury caused by your work — often required for city registration or subcontractor approval.

✓

Workers' compensation

Required in both Illinois and Iowa for employers with employees, and often specifically for construction work.

✓

Commercial auto

Personal auto policies typically don't cover work vehicles used for business — commercial coverage is usually needed.

✓

License & contractor bonds

Certain state licenses (like Illinois roofing) and Iowa plumbing/mechanical licensing require a surety bond as a condition of licensure.

Why this matters for marketing: "licensed and insured" is one of the most-searched phrases for home service businesses — but it should only appear on your site if it's actually true, for the specific license and coverage you hold.

Why licensing matters before you build a website or run ads.

An inaccurate "licensed and insured" claim can cost you a job — or worse, a customer's trust — once it's discovered.

✓

Don't imply services you can't perform

Your website shouldn't list services, trades, or service areas you're not currently licensed or registered to perform.

✓

Accuracy supports conversion

Accurate license numbers, service areas, insurance claims, and guarantees build the kind of trust that turns visitors into booked jobs.

Before you market your business

  • Confirm your LLC or corporation is properly registered.
  • Check whether your trade requires a state license in Illinois or Iowa.
  • Check whether your city or county requires separate contractor registration.
  • Confirm you have the right insurance — general liability, workers' comp, auto.
  • Confirm any required bonds are in place before advertising.
  • Make sure your website only lists services and areas you're currently qualified to perform.

Questions, answered

Contractor licensing FAQ.

Do I need more than an LLC to run a contractor business in Illinois?

Usually yes. An LLC registers your business entity with the state, but most trades also need state or local licensing, city registration, permits, and insurance before performing or advertising work.

Do Iowa contractors need to register with the state?

Yes. Iowa requires individuals and businesses earning $2,000 or more per year from construction work to register as a Construction Contractor with the Iowa Department of Inspections, Appeals, and Licensing (DIAL).

Do plumbers need a license in Illinois and Iowa?

Yes in both states. Illinois licenses plumbers statewide through the Illinois Department of Public Health. Iowa licenses plumbing and mechanical contractors through the Iowa Plumbing and Mechanical Systems Board.

Does Illinois have a general contractor license?

No. Illinois does not issue a statewide general contractor license. General contracting, electrical, and HVAC work are regulated at the city or county level, such as Chicago's Department of Buildings.

Do landscapers need a license to apply pesticides?

Typically yes. Applying pesticides or herbicides for hire generally requires a commercial pesticide applicator license through the state department of agriculture in both Illinois and Iowa.

Do roofers need a license in Illinois?

Yes. Illinois requires a statewide roofing contractor license through the Illinois Department of Financial and Professional Regulation under the Roofing Industry Licensing Act.

Do electricians need a state license in Iowa?

Yes. Iowa licenses electrical contractors and electricians through the Iowa Electrical Examining Board, separate from general construction contractor registration.

Can I advertise contractor services before getting licensed?

You should hold any required license or registration before advertising services that legally require one. Advertising services you're not licensed to perform can create legal and reputational risk.

Should I put my license number on my website?

If you hold a license, an accurate license number can build trust. Never display a license number you don't currently hold, or one that doesn't cover the specific work being advertised.

Do city permits matter if I already have an LLC?

Yes. An LLC is a business entity registration and does not replace city or county permits, inspections, or local contractor registration required for specific projects.

What insurance should a home service contractor carry?

Most contractors carry general liability insurance, workers' compensation if they have employees, commercial auto coverage, and sometimes a license or contractor bond depending on the trade and jurisdiction.
